01 · Problem

The Problem Worth Solving

A leading global job platform was losing recruiter trust. Despite strong market position, the platform wasn't working well for a critical user segment — recruiters hiring lightly-skilled workers like telecallers, sales reps, and admin assistants.

The hiring cycle was too long. Matches were poor. Recruiters were abandoning the platform mid-process. But the client didn't know if it was a product problem, a behavior problem, or a workflow mismatch.

Our brief: get inside the minds of recruiters. Understand their mental models, decision-making, and daily frustrations — then evaluate whether a new product feature actually solved the right problem.

On average, a recruiter spends 13 hours manually sourcing candidates for a single role. The platform wasn't reducing that burden — it was adding to it.

— Research finding, 6-week recruiter study
13h

Lost to Manual Sourcing Per Role

Recruiters were spending 13 hours per role on manual efforts — calls, WhatsApp messages, and follow-ups — because the platform couldn't support their actual workflow. This wasn't a user education problem. It was a product gap.

02 · Approach

My Approach — and Why

1
Ran Deep IDIs Before Touching the Prototype
Before testing the product solution, I needed to understand the problem space deeply. I ran in-depth remote interviews to map mental models, hiring workflows, and communication patterns across all three recruiter types.
→ Revealed that three recruiter segments had fundamentally different workflows — meaning one product solution couldn't serve them all equally.
2
Used Prototype Testing to Validate, Not Just Explore
Once we understood the problem space, I ran prototype evaluation using the Think Aloud technique. Recruiters interacted with a new candidate management feature in real time, surfacing usability gaps and workflow mismatches live.
→ Uncovered that the prototype didn't match recruiter working styles — despite solving the right problem in theory.
3
Built Recruiter Personas, Not Just User Feedback
Rather than synthesizing generic pain points, I structured findings into 3 distinct recruiter profiles — each with different workflows, communication preferences, and platform needs. This made recommendations directly actionable for the product team.
→ Gave the PM team a framework to prioritize features by segment rather than building for an average user that doesn't exist.
4
Created a DILO to Show the Full Hiring Context
A Day-in-the-Life-Of map captured the full recruiter journey — from initial sourcing through to post-joining follow-up — including the devices, communication tools, and workarounds they used outside the platform.
→ Showed the client where the platform fit (and didn't fit) into real recruiter workflows — critical for product prioritization.

03 · Findings

What the Research Revealed

01
The platform wasn't reducing manual effort — it was adding to it
Product implication13 hours per role on manual sourcing means the platform's core value proposition wasn't landing. Features need to reduce clicks and communication steps, not add them.
02
Three recruiter types have fundamentally incompatible workflow needs
Product implicationSegment-specific feature prioritization is required. A single interface optimized for "all recruiters" will fail all three. Business owners need fewer steps; HR agencies need pipeline depth.
03
WhatsApp and phone calls do the work the platform should be doing
Product implicationRecruiters defaulted to external tools because in-app communication was inadequate. Opportunity: bring candidate communication into the platform with WhatsApp-like simplicity.
04
Cognitive load from cluttered UI was causing drop-off mid-process
Product implicationMaking important information more visible and reducing interface complexity could directly improve hiring completion rates. Information hierarchy is the primary UX fix needed.
05
Recruiters liked the prototype concept — but not the execution
Product implicationSegregated tabs and the calling feature were directionally right. But the flow didn't match how recruiters actually worked. Needs fewer clicks and better alignment to existing mental models.
Research Artifacts — Recruiter Personas
Recruiter Segments — 3 Profiles Identified
Business Owner
Speed over depth
Wears multiple hats. Hiring is one of many responsibilities. Needs a streamlined, minimal-click process — doesn't have time for complex workflows or multi-step candidate management.
HR Agency Owner
Pipeline visibility first
Manages multiple clients and candidates simultaneously. Monitors pipeline progress and follows up post-joining for up to 2 months. Revenue depends on successful placements.
HR Professional at SMB
Process & communication
Follows a rigorous, multi-step hiring process. Keeps candidates engaged throughout. Needs robust communication tools, progress tracking, and clear candidate status visibility across the funnel.

04 · Prototype Testing

What the Prototype Testing Told Us

We tested a new candidate management feature designed to streamline how recruiters handled lightly-skilled job seekers. Using the Think Aloud technique, recruiters interacted with the prototype in real time — narrating their reactions, expectations, and frustrations as they went.

The goal wasn't just usability feedback. It was to understand whether the feature concept matched recruiter mental models — and if not, what it would take to get there.

The test surfaced a critical gap: the prototype solved the right problem, but the execution didn't fit how recruiters actually work. They liked the direction. They couldn't use it efficiently.

Segregated tabs were the right call
Recruiters responded positively to separated views for calls vs. applications — it matched their mental model of managing two distinct candidate pools.
In-app calling feature was directionally right
Recruiters welcomed the idea of calling from within the platform — reducing the context-switching they currently do between the app and their phone.
Flow didn't match working styles
Too many clicks to complete common tasks. Recruiters — especially business owners — abandoned mid-flow. The prototype needed a tighter action hierarchy.
Candidate information wasn't visible enough
Key details needed to make a call decision were buried. Recruiters wanted candidate essentials surfaced immediately — not after tapping through multiple screens.
05 · Outcome

What Changed

The client used our findings to close the loop between what employers needed and what the platform was actually offering — and to prioritize their product roadmap accordingly.
→
Generic users → 3 distinct segments The product team moved from building for "all recruiters" to segment-specific feature prioritization across business owners, agencies, and SMB HR teams.
→
Feature request backlog → Research-backed roadmap Findings gave the PM team a clear framework: reduce cognitive load first, improve communication tools second, add advanced features third.
→
Prototype concept → Actionable redesign brief Prototype testing turned abstract feedback into specific UI fixes — fewer clicks, surfaced candidate info, better flow alignment with recruiter working styles.

Prototype Evaluation

07 · Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Research and product work best when they talk to each other earlier.

The main lesson from this project: the gap between research insight and product decision is where value gets lost. I'd close that gap faster next time.

Involve the PM team in synthesis, not just the final output. The product team received insights at the end. Had they been in the room during synthesis, they would have asked sharper questions and the recommendations would have been more precisely targeted to their actual roadmap decisions.

Run prototype testing iteratively, not as a single round. One round of testing caught important issues, but a second iteration with revised designs would have validated whether our recommendations actually improved the experience — closing the loop between research and product.

Quantify the cost of recruiter drop-off more sharply. We surfaced the 13-hour manual sourcing finding qualitatively. A quick analysis of platform completion rates by recruiter segment would have made the business case for prioritizing UX simplification much harder to ignore.
