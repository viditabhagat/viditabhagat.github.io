Rebuilding an AI Robotics Company's Entire Identity — From the Ground Up

When General Robotics pivoted its mission, the old brand couldn't carry the new vision. The goal was to rebuilt everything: identity, design system, architecture, and the live website.

01 · Problem

The Problem Worth Solving

General Robotics — formerly Scaled Foundation — had fundamentally changed what it was building. The new mission: general-purpose intelligence for every robot, across any form, task, or environment. But the name, brand, and website still reflected the old company.

For a deep-tech AI startup pitching investors and enterprise clients, brand credibility is product credibility. If the website can't communicate the vision clearly in 10 seconds, the meeting doesn't happen. The document doesn't get read. The deal doesn't close.

The brief was to rebuild everything and to do it in a way that made a genuinely complex technical concept feel immediately legible to a non-technical decision-maker.

Investors and enterprise clients don't read websites, they judge them. The design challenge wasn't explaining modular robot AI. It was making people feel the company's credibility before they understood the technology.

02 · Approach

What I Built — End to End

1
Brand Archetype & Personality — The Sage
Defined General Robotics as The Sage — authoritative, research-led, intelligent. A brand that leads with expertise rather than hype. Not a startup trying to look big. A serious technical company whose credibility is earned through knowledge, rigour, and safety-first thinking.
→ Established the tonal foundation everything else was built on — from copy to visual hierarchy.
2
Design System — Built on the New Identity
The visual identity (logo, colour palette, typography) was developed by an external agency. My job was to take that identity and build a complete, functional design system from it, translating brand decisions into reusable components, spacing rules, and layout patterns that could actually ship.
→ Closed the gap between a beautiful brand document and a working, buildable product.
3
Brand Communication Identity
Defined how the brand speaks — tone, vocabulary, and messaging hierarchy. Technical enough to be credible to engineers, clear enough for a VP of Operations to understand immediately. The Sage doesn't hype, doesn't hedge, and doesn't explain what doesn't need explaining.
→ Gave the whole team a shared vocabulary for writing consistently across all touchpoints.
4
Component Library
Built a reusable UI component library — buttons, cards, sections, layout primitives so the design could scale consistently as the company added new pages and content without starting from scratch each time.
→ Made future design additions faster and more consistent without a design brief for every new page.
5
Site Architecture
Designed the information architecture from the ground up. Made structural decisions about what deserved a page, what belonged on the homepage, and how to guide two very different audiences — developers and enterprise buyers — through the same site without losing either.
→ The radical restraint (very few pages) became a feature, not a limitation.
6
Built & Published in Webflow
Took the designs from concept to live site. Implemented every page in Webflow, refined in-browser, and published — meaning I was responsible for design fidelity, performance, and the final shipped product. No handoff. The designer is the developer.
→ Compressed the design-to-launch cycle and eliminated translation errors between design and engineering.

03 · Findings

The Design Decisions That Mattered

01
Dark, minimal aesthetic — not the typical AI startup look
Why this was the right callThe robotics and defence-adjacent AI space skews dark, technical, precise. A clean dark palette with restrained typography communicated seriousness and credibility — matching the expectations of enterprise procurement teams and institutional investors, not consumer app users.
02
Two explicit CTAs for two distinct audiences
Why this was the right callDevelopers access GRID directly (Login). Enterprise clients need a conversation (Contact Sales). Forcing both audiences through the same CTA would have confused intent and diluted conversion. Splitting them clarified the product structure and reduced friction for both.
03
Team credibility surfaced above the fold
Why this was the right callFor an early-stage deep-tech startup, the team IS the product. The founders built AirSim, contributed to VideoPoet and TensorFlow Object Detection — Microsoft and Google Research pedigrees. Surfacing this early converts investor scepticism before it forms.
04
Very few pages — radical restraint on content
Why this was the right callInvestors and enterprise clients don't browse. They scan the homepage and decide. More pages create more chances to lose someone. The constraint became a strategic advantage — forcing every word to earn its place and making the site feel confident rather than defensive.
05
GRID explained as two tiers, not one product
Why this was the right callOpen GRID (free, for developers) and GRID Enterprise (paid, for large organisations) serve different needs and budgets. Presenting them as one product would have confused pricing signals. Separating them clearly reduced support friction and improved conversion for both tiers.

06 · Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Designing alone moves fast. It also has blind spots.

Working solo with one feedback loop — the founder and manager — gave me speed and creative ownership. It also meant some things only became visible after launch.

Test the site with real investors and enterprise clients before launch. I designed based on my understanding of the audience — but I didn't test with actual VCs or enterprise procurement teams. A single round of moderated testing with 3–4 people from each audience would have surfaced misalignments I couldn't see from the inside.

Build the design system with handoff in mind from day one. The design system I built worked well for me. But as the company grows and brings on engineers or other designers, the component documentation needs to be more explicit. I'd invest more in annotation and usage guidelines upfront.

Establish baseline metrics before launch, not after. We captured post-launch numbers, but I didn't set up proper pre-launch benchmarks from the old site. Having a clean before/after comparison would have made the impact story considerably stronger for stakeholders — and for me as a designer.
