01 · Problem

The Problem Worth Solving

Hypertension affects 1 in 4 adults in India — yet 1 in 3 people with high blood pressure don't know they have it. Our client, a global pharmaceutical leader, knew their products worked clinically. What they didn't understand was why patients weren't staying on treatment.

The real question underneath the brief was harder: where in the patient journey could they intervene most effectively, and what would actually change behavior?

This wasn't a standard usability study. We were mapping a 10+ year disease journey across patients with multiple comorbidities, varying health literacy levels, and wildly different access to healthcare — across both metros and Tier 2 cities.

The biggest barrier to hypertension management in India wasn't medication efficacy. It was a systemic breakdown in awareness, diagnosis, and behavioral adherence long before a patient ever saw a doctor.

— Core finding, 6-month ethnographic study
02 · Approach

My Approach — and Why

1
Chose ethnographic over survey methods
Home visits and hospital observations rather than questionnaires — because patients consistently underreport adherence issues in clinical settings.
→ Being in their homes showed us what actually happens at 8pm when they're tired and have 6 pills to take.
2
Recruited across comorbidity complexity
Included patients with hypertension alongside diabetes, heart failure, and stroke — because that's the actual patient reality, not a cleaned-up sample.
→ Surfaced pill burden insights that a standard sample would have missed entirely.
3
Ran HCPs as a parallel stream
Talked to GPs, cardiologists, neurologists, and nurses simultaneously with patients — not as an afterthought.
→ Mapped the critical tension between what doctors prescribe and what patients actually do.
4
Synthesized around behavior drivers
Structured synthesis around underlying behavioral and systemic drivers — not just pain points — because those are what product interventions can actually address.
→ Made the output directly actionable for the client's strategy team.
Research in the Field

Field research across Tier 1 & Tier 2 cities — home visits, hospital observations, and remote in-depth interviews with patients, caregivers, and HCPs.

03 · Findings

Strategic Opportunities Found

01
The awareness gap is earlier than expected
So what Patients fail at recognition before they fail at adherence. Opportunity: upstream education interventions before diagnosis.
02
Young hypertension is a growing crisis being ignored
So what Current materials are built for older demographics. Under-35 patients experience intense denial post-diagnosis. Opportunity: age-segmented communication.
03
The kitchen is the real treatment center
So what Female caregivers are leading dietary management — often with beliefs that contradict medical advice. Opportunity: family-centered intervention design.
04
Pill burden is cognitively underestimated by HCPs
So what Patients quietly drop medications to simplify their regimen. Opportunity: simplified regimen solutions and caregiver support tools.
05
Monitoring gaps break the behavioral feedback loop
So what When patients feel fine, they stop medication. BP monitoring creates the reinforcement loop. Opportunity: accessible home monitoring + digital reminders.
06
Misinformation undermines trust in medication
So what Fear of side effects drives silent treatment abandonment. Opportunity: proactive, culturally-grounded safety communication.
07
HCP-patient communication breaks down at diagnosis
So what Patients leave diagnosis consultations confused and without behavioral guidance. Opportunity: tools to support better diagnostic conversations.

Patient Personas — 5 Profiles
The Adherent
Controlled
Disciplined, routine-driven. Takes medication consistently. Needs reinforcement and long-term engagement.
Anxious Navigator
Anxious
Over-researches, fears side effects. High engagement but prone to misinformation spirals.
The Denier
Uncontrolled
Asymptomatic nature fuels denial. Delays treatment. High risk. Hardest to reach.
Lost Wanderer
Disengaged
Overwhelmed by complexity of managing multiple conditions. Falls off treatment without active support.
Reckless Rebel
High Risk
Young, dismissive. Lifestyle-first. Treats hypertension as inconvenient, not dangerous.

HCP

04 · Outcome

What Changed

Our findings shifted the client from a treatment-focused to a journey-focused product strategy — and reshaped their India roadmap.
→
Treatment-focused → Journey-focused Biggest value-creation opportunity moved upstream, before formal diagnosis.
→
Patient-only → Family-centered Caregiver education incorporated into product and communication strategy.
→
Demographic-generic → Persona-specific Behavioral personas used to segment and target interventions across the patient journey.
05 · Role

Field Research
Moderated 80%+ of qualitative sessions across home visits, hospital observations, and remote IDIs across Tier 1 and Tier 2 cities.
Synthesis & Strategy

06 · Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Honest reflection builds better researchers.

Every project leaves something on the table. Here's what I'd do differently if I ran this again.

Share preliminary insights mid-study, not just at the end. A co-creation session with the client's product team halfway through fieldwork would have let us sharpen our focus in the second phase rather than waiting until synthesis to course-correct.

Build in a quantitative validation layer. The qualitative depth was strong, but a quick survey to test prevalence of key findings across a larger sample would have strengthened the business case for roadmap changes.

Document regional variations more systematically. The differences between cities were significant and influential — but somewhat underrepresented in the final synthesis given the volume of data.
