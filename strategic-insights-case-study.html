01 · Problem

The Problem Worth Solving

A UK-based innovation accelerator was preparing to enter the Indian EV mobility market. They came in with a well-defined hypothesis: urban professionals in metro cities were their primary target — people who commuted daily, owned smartphones, and were already familiar with ride-hailing apps. The product roadmap, pricing model, and go-to-market strategy had all been built around this imagined user.

The brief asked for validation research. But as I began the project, it became clear that what was needed wasn't validation — it was a genuine interrogation of the assumption itself. The client had built a picture of their user from desk research and analogous markets. No one had actually spoken to people in Kolkata yet.

The core question I designed toward was: Who actually moves through this city, how, and why? And — critically — who is currently underserved by existing mobility options in ways that an EV solution might address? The answer turned out to be almost no one the client had expected.

The client arrived with a clear picture of who their users were. Eight weeks later, that picture had completely changed — and the clarity they left with was built on what people actually said and did, not what the client had hoped to hear.

— Core finding, EV Mobility Field Study, Kolkata

The Twist That Made This Project Matter

The client assumed their users were tech-savvy urban professionals who would adopt EVs for convenience. What we found instead was that the people most constrained by current mobility options — and most open to an alternative — were delivery workers, students on long intra-city commutes, and informal-sector workers managing unpredictable schedules. Not a single person in the original target profile appeared in our data as a plausible early adopter.

02 · Approach

My Approach — and Why

1
Contextual Inquiry in Transit Hubs and Neighborhoods
Rather than recruiting participants and bringing them to an interview room, I conducted research where mobility decisions actually happen — at autorickshaw stands, outside metro exits, near markets and colleges. I spent time observing before approaching, and asked people to narrate what they were doing and why in the moment.
→ Revealed the gap between stated preference (comfort, speed) and actual behavior (cost-optimized, multi-modal, highly improvised) — a gap that would have been invisible in a standard interview setting.
2
Day-In-The-Life Shadowing Across Four User Types
I identified four emergent user archetypes early in fieldwork and recruited one participant from each to shadow through a full mobility day — from first trip to last. This gave me end-to-end visibility into the real texture of urban mobility: the waiting, the switching, the negotiations, the workarounds.
→ Uncovered that "trip planning" is largely mythological for most users. Decisions are made in motion, under constraint — which has direct implications for how any EV product must present itself at point of need.
3
Assumption Mapping Workshop with the Client Team
Mid-project, before sharing findings, I ran a 90-minute remote workshop with the client's product and strategy team to surface and articulate all their current assumptions about the Indian market. I then used this as a direct comparison layer when presenting field data — showing assumption versus observed reality for each belief.
→ Made the gap between hypothesis and evidence concrete and undeniable, rather than abstract. The client team experienced the misalignment directly, which made the pivot recommendations far more actionable than a report alone would have been.

Research in the Field — Kolkata, 8 Weeks

Fieldwork conducted across south, central, and north Kolkata over 8 weeks — transit hubs, markets, residential areas, and commute corridors.

03 · Findings

Strategic Opportunities Found

01
The assumed primary user — the tech-savvy urban professional — is already well-served by existing options and shows low unmet need
So whatThis group uses Ola, Uber, and Rapido with reasonable satisfaction. Their switching threshold is high. Targeting them as early adopters requires significant price and convenience advantages that a new entrant cannot realistically offer at launch.
02
Informal and gig workers have the highest daily mobility load but the least reliable access to transport — they are the most underserved segment in the market
So whatDelivery workers, domestic workers, and informal vendors make 4–7 trips per day across variable routes. They rely on an improvised mix of modes with frequent service failure. A reliable, affordable EV option for this segment would address a genuine gap — and creates natural density for a network effect.
03
Trust in a new mobility service is built around driver relationships and route familiarity — not brand or app quality
So whatUsers mentioned specific drivers, tuk-tuk uncles, and auto stands they return to by name. The relationship layer is the product. Any EV service entering this market must invest in driver onboarding and community presence before optimizing the app experience.
04
Range anxiety is not about battery technology — it is about uncertainty in an already uncertain daily experience
So whatParticipants didn't reject EVs on technical grounds. They rejected additional unpredictability. Charging infrastructure visibility and reliable service uptime are the actual adoption barriers — not skepticism about electric vehicles as a concept.
05
Students on long intra-city commutes represent an untapped, high-frequency, cost-sensitive segment with strong potential for habit formation
So whatCollege students make predictable, repeated trips between campus and home over 3–5 year periods. They are price-sensitive but open to new options if reliability is demonstrated. Winning this segment early could establish behavioral lock-in before competitors reach them.

04 · Outcome

What Changed

Our findings shifted the client from a validation mindset to a discovery posture — and from a target segment that didn't exist to one that genuinely needed them.
→
From urban professional to gig and informal worker The primary go-to-market target was revised entirely. The client's new ICP — informal and gig economy workers with high daily trip frequency and low current reliability — represented both a genuine unmet need and a viable network density play.
→
From app-first to trust-first product strategy The product roadmap deprioritized app UX improvements in favor of driver community investment, neighborhood-level presence, and visible charging infrastructure as the primary early adoption drivers.
→
From broad metro launch to corridor-specific pilot Rather than a city-wide launch, the client committed to a single high-density commute corridor as a pilot — chosen specifically because it served both the student segment and the delivery worker segment in the same geography.
05 · Role

My Specific Role

Research Design & Fieldwork Lead
I designed the full research plan — including method selection, participant recruitment criteria, field protocols, and the assumption-mapping workshop. I conducted all fieldwork personally, including the DILO shadowing sessions across all four user archetypes.
Analysis & Synthesis
I analyzed over 40 hours of field notes, audio recordings, and observational data to identify the five strategic insights. I built the personas, DILO maps, and a comparative assumption-vs-reality framework to structure the final presentation.
Strategic Recommendation & Client Delivery
I presented findings to the client's founding team and investors in a two-hour workshop. I framed each insight as a strategic decision point, not just a data point, and facilitated the session to close with a revised ICP and a concrete pilot corridor commitment.
06 · Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

Research that contradicts is harder to deliver than research that confirms.

The most important skill in this project wasn't finding the insight — it was structuring the delivery so the client could receive it without becoming defensive. I learned more about research communication here than in any other project.

Start assumption-mapping earlier. I ran the assumption workshop mid-project, but doing it in week one would have sharpened my field questions considerably. Having the client's hypotheses documented upfront would have let me design specific probes to test each one, rather than surfacing contradictions retrospectively.

Involve the client in field observation sooner. The most productive moments in the final workshop were when I could say "you saw this too." Even one day of client-side observation during week two would have built shared understanding that no amount of reporting could replicate. Next time, I'd design this in from the start.

Build a richer competitive landscape before fieldwork begins. I spent time mid-study understanding the existing mobility ecosystem in Kolkata — operators, pricing, route density — that I should have done in the first week. It would have made my field questions more targeted and my eventual recommendations more grounded in market reality.
