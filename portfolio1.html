Project Context

Until recently, the shared mobility market was largely untapped all across the globe when suddenly, it began to grow rapidly, especially in the form of two-wheeler electric vehicle (EV) rental services. Recognizing the importance of electrifying vehicle fleets in advancing this sector, a UK-based startup saw an opportunity.

The client wanted to explore the Indian market, aiming to introduce two-wheeler EV rentals and thus, it became important to understand the market's readiness and feasibility for such a venture. To achieve this, they embarked on a research project to gather insights and evaluate the potential for success.

The goal was to provide a roadmap to enter the Indian market and seize opportunities in the burgeoning two-wheeler EV rental space. This research would pave the way for establishing successful businesses, bridging the gap between innovative ideas and practical implementation in the dynamic landscape of India's shared mobility sector.

Project Planning

We set out to uncover the unique experiences, challenges and aspirations of prospective and existing users from diverse user profiles (from professional workers to delivery drivers, from students to stay-at-home) engaging with 2EV rentals in New Town, Kolkata.

The main objective was to identify the scope for new players to enter into the EV mobility space in the Indian market including:

  • Identifying the target user segment
  • Their needs & habits, mental models, challenges, and preferences
  • Understanding different use cases and travel patterns
  • Opportunities for innovation in the Product design of the vehicle
  • To help the client develop a replicable, contextual, scaleable 2W EV rental service model and business model for the Indian context

Breaking Boundaries: Blending Reseach Methods

Remote In-depth Interviews (IDI)

To reach a broad spectrum of users, we conducted Remote In-Depth Interviews (IDIs). This method allowed us to engage with prospective users from various socio-economic backgrounds. However, we recognized that users' experiences were shaped not only by the digital components—such as the app—but also by the physical components, including the bike and infrastructure.
During these interviews, we also incorporated Day-In-The-Life (DILO) discussions, where users described their daily interactions with the 2W EV rental service. This provided a comprehensive view of how the service fit into their routines, uncovering pain points and areas for improvement.
The specific limitation of Remote IDIs in the context of this study was that it did not enable us to make in-situ observations of the existing users’ interaction with the service as the user journey switched from digital (booking bike) to digital+physical (unlocking and locking bike) to only physical (riding bike).

Field Study

Realizing the limitations of remote interviews, our team hit the streets of New Town for a hands-on field study. We observed users in their natural environment, noting how they interacted with the 2W EV rental service. This on-the-ground research was crucial for understanding the practical aspects of service utilization, such as planning and managing infrastructure. We discovered the mismatch between the digital and physical realms of the service, observing users' frustrations and their creative solutions to these challenges. These real-life interactions offered a depth of insight that remote interviews could not.

Approach

Our approach went beyond evaluating how people used the existing solution. We delved into users' motivations, deepest needs, and expectations from a mobility experience. This strategic approach aimed to uncover the actual problem space and identify untapped opportunities. By immersing ourselves in the lives of both prospective and current users, we aimed to bring forth what they needed and expected from the product and service. Consequently, our research design focused on recruiting more prospective users (63%) compared to current users (36%).

Through this immersive research, we learned that the 'imaginary' users in the client's list were not a reality. Our unconventional approach helped identify and separate 'real' user groups from 'assumed' ones.
