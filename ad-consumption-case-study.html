Unlocking Why Users Don't Click — and What a Unicorn Platform Could Do About It

How eye tracking in users' homes — not a lab — revealed the real behavioral drivers behind low ad engagement on a leading Indian social media platform.

01 · Problem

The Problem Worth Solving

A leading Indian social media and entertainment unicorn had a revenue problem. Despite strong user numbers, their click-through rates and ad engagement were significantly below industry average — even after improvements to features and UX.

The platform's internal analysis couldn't explain the gap. Was it ad fatigue? Irrelevance? Lack of trust? Or something deeper in how users actually consumed content?

Our brief: decode the behavioral reality of ad consumption — not what users said they did, but what they actually did — and identify strategies to improve ad performance that aligned with real user behavior.

74% of users express fatigue with social media ads — yet ads remain one of the most effective marketing tools when matched to the right context and moment. The gap isn't the ads. It's the targeting of attention.

— CMO survey finding that shaped our research design

The methodological decision that made this study different

Most eye tracking research happens in labs — controlled, artificial, and disconnected from real usage behavior. We ran eye tracking in participants' own homes, as they used the app in their natural environment. This meant we captured actual attention patterns — rapid scrolling, distraction, habitual consumption — not performance behavior in a sterile setting.

02 · Approach

My Approach — and Why

1
In-depth interviews to understand context and culture
We conducted in-person IDIs across 9 cities spanning Tier 1, 2, and 3 India — capturing how regional, cultural, and socioeconomic factors shaped how users related to ads and content. Interviews were conducted in 5 languages to build genuine rapport.
→ Surfaced that linguistic connection was critical — participants shared far more when spoken to in their own language.
2
Home visits to observe real-world app usage
Field visits from Veeranam to Mumbai let us observe how users actually engaged with the app at home — not how they described using it. We saw habitual scrolling behavior, multi-tasking patterns, and the real contexts in which ads appeared in their daily routines.
→ Revealed that rapid scrolling was causing users to completely miss ads — a behavioral pattern invisible in interviews.
3
Eye tracking deployed in homes, not labs
We integrated eye-tracking technology in participants' home environments rather than a conventional lab setting. Gaze plots and heat maps generated precise, contextually accurate data on where attention actually landed during content consumption and ad exposure.
→ Captured authentic attention patterns that lab settings systematically miss — including scroll-induced ad blindness.
Eye Tracking — How It Works

Why eye tracking mattered for this brief specifically. The client's hypothesis was about low CTR — a metric that only tells you what happened, not why. Did users see the ad and choose not to click? Or did they never see it at all?

Eye tracking answered that question precisely. Gaze plots showed us exactly where attention landed frame by frame. Heat maps visualized density of attention across the screen. Together they revealed something the CTR data couldn't: a significant portion of users were scrolling past ads before their eyes even registered them.

This reframed the entire problem — from "ads aren't compelling" to "ads aren't being seen." Two very different product and sales strategy implications.

03 · Findings

What the Research Revealed

01
Rapid scrolling meant many ads were never seen at all
Sales strategy implicationLow CTR isn't a creative problem — it's a visibility problem. Ad placement strategy needs to account for scroll velocity. Interruption-based formats outperform passive feed ads for this user base.
02
Delayed CTAs in video ads caused missed interactions
Sales strategy implicationWhen the call-to-action appeared too late in a video, users had already scrolled past. CTA placement in the first 3 seconds dramatically increases interaction likelihood for this platform's usage pattern.
03
Engagement was driven by genre, celebrity, or learning value — not brand
Sales strategy implicationAds that matched users' content preferences in genre or featured recognizable faces outperformed generic brand creatives. Native content-style ads significantly outperform traditional formats.
04
Users couldn't distinguish native ads from network ads
Sales strategy implicationVisual similarity between ad types created confusion. Users who couldn't identify an ad as native were less likely to engage. Clearer but non-intrusive ad labeling could improve trust and click intent.
05
Ads were viewed as distractions, with low recall and near-zero purchase intent
Sales strategy implicationThe platform's ad revenue model can't rely on direct conversion — it needs to optimize for awareness and contextual relevance instead. Advertiser pitch strategy needs to shift accordingly.
04 · Outcome

What Changed

Our research reframed the client's problem from "ads aren't compelling" to "ads aren't being seen" — fundamentally shifting how they approached their sales strategy and ad product design.
→
Creative problem → Placement & timing problem The client shifted focus from ad creative quality to ad placement mechanics — scroll-aware formats, early CTAs, and interruption-based placements for high scroll-velocity users.
→
Generic ad formats → Context-matched creatives Sales team repositioned advertiser briefs around genre-matching and celebrity-led content — formats that eye tracking proved actually captured user attention.
→
Conversion-focused pitch → Awareness-first pitch Low purchase intent data gave the sales team evidence to reframe advertiser expectations — shifting from direct conversion metrics to brand awareness and contextual reach.

06 · Reflection

What I'd Do Differently

The richest data comes when you remove every layer of artificiality.

This project reinforced that behavioral research is only as good as the context it's conducted in. What I'd push harder on next time.

Recruit more female participants from Tier 3 cities specifically. Our sample skewed male and urban in some segments. The ad consumption patterns of women in Tier 3 cities — given their different content preferences and platform usage times — would have added significant depth to the findings.

Map behavioral findings more directly to ad revenue metrics. We surfaced strong behavioral insights, but connecting them explicitly to CTR uplift estimates or revenue impact would have given the sales strategy team a much stronger business case for acting on each recommendation.

Include a longitudinal component for ad recall. We measured in-session attention and immediate recall. A follow-up session 24–48 hours later to test delayed recall would have given the client stronger data on brand awareness impact — the metric they ultimately needed to sell to advertisers.
